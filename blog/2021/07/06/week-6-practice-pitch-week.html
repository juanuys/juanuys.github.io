Core to the pitch is this:

Just to be clear:

  • what they want: a return on investment, i.e. more money than they put in

I‚Äôve worked on a few pitches of my own over the years:

Nail the vision and the logline

Before you start a pitch, you start with why (Sinek and Sinek 2015). You need to be able to articulate why you want to create a particular IP, and come up with (and agree on, as a team) a coherent vision (GDC n.d.).

Iterate on, and nail down the logline (a one-sentence 30-ish word summary or description of the game) (‚ÄúScreenwriting Tips: How to Write a Logline - 2021‚Äù n.d.), as this will be the initial hook that sets your audience up for the rest of the presentation. (Loglines are also something I‚Äôve had a bit of practice writing in a previous course.)

What goes in the pitch

The pitch document itself has to cover these facets (Schell 2019: 526), (Hacura 2021), (GDC n.d.):

  • state the platform, audience, and genre
  • don‚Äôt bore them with your game‚Äôs story (GDC n.d.), but instead convey the fantasy your game fullfills: who am I, where am I, what am I trying to do (‚ÄúGDC Talk: How To Explain Your Game To An Asshole - a Post on Tom Francis‚Äô Blog‚Äù n.d.)?
  • state the ‚ÄúX for/with Y‚Äù, or give your game ‚Äúhandles‚Äù, e.g. DOOM but with geriatrics
  • show, don‚Äôt tell. Have the prototype ready. From highest to lowest importance (where available): video with greatest hits, a working prototype, screenshots, mockups. BTW, if you do show a prototype, don‚Äôt show the easy stuff, but show the USPs (e.g. your character can open a door) or show the stuff which is mitigating your risks (GDC n.d.).
  • make your pitch pitchable by them. Once someone in the room is hooked, they need to go an sell it to their superiors, and you won‚Äôt always be on hand.
  • know all the design details, like how many hours of gameplay, how long does it take to finish a level, etc
  • know the schedule, how long it will take to make (this will depend on where you are with the game, e.g. still in pre-production), and when each milestone will be hit (e.g. first playable, alpha, beta, launch, etc)
  • financials. How much will production cost? Break down how many people are on it, how many hours they‚Äôre on it. How much money will the game make? Base the answer on comparable titles, and give a reasonable range.
  • state the risks. Like technical, legal, etc. Also definitely state how we‚Äôre mitigating risks.
  • introduce the team. The investors are not just investing in the idea, but mostly the people. If it‚Äôs a large team, focus on the core team, the heavy hitters. If you don‚Äôt have a team, have a roadmap of how you‚Äôre going to use the funding to hire the right people. Have a team (GDC n.d.)!
  • when you show art, show great art. If not, show very obvious placeholder graphics, otherwise bad art might be mistaken for your vision or final art (GDC n.d.).
  • select the right/appropriate audience (e.g. don‚Äôt pitch a mobile game to a console publisher), and then know your audience (e.g. don‚Äôt pitch a walking sim to an FPS publisher, or if they‚Äôre already making a walking sim), and then know the individuals in the panel (don‚Äôt be a stalker, but it helps to win over individuals with carefully crafted content)

And very essential to the pitch are: is this game worth making, and can this team make it (GDC n.d.)?

Pitch tips

(Mostly from ‚Äú30 things I hate about your pitch‚Äù (GDC n.d.).)

  • Be nice. The publisher/developer relationship is going to be a long one, so give them assurance they‚Äôre going to be working with someone nice
  • Be humble. Not everyone knows who you are, or may have played your previous games.
  • Don‚Äôt get annoyed when they ask questions. You should be happy, as they‚Äôre interested, and it gives you another opportunity to be clear and explain something.
  • Make the pitch accessible. Large projector, or if remote, have a clear high-res video. Speak clearly.
  • Bring headphones (if the pitch area is going to be noisy), because audio is important too. (Don‚Äôt bring earbuds - they won‚Äôt put it in their ears.)
  • Be sharp. Dress the part. Be clean, smell nice. Don‚Äôt be drunk, hungover, or high.
  • Be professional. Don‚Äôt trash other companies or games. (Be it competing developers, or publishers whom you‚Äôve worked with or turned you down before.)

Convey these 4 things (‚ÄúGDC Talk: How To Explain Your Game To An Asshole - a Post on Tom Francis‚Äô Blog‚Äù n.d.):

  • type of game, e.g. ‚Äú3D underwater exploration game‚Äù
  • coolest thing about it, e.g. ‚Äúyou can reverse time‚Äù
  • give context/fantasy
  • give an example of how it plays, e.g. not ‚Äúyou possess NPCs‚Äù but rather ‚ÄúI can possess an enemy, throw him into a friend, and knock them both into a landmine before I switch back to my own body and watch them blow up‚Äù

Finally, be enthusiastic, honest. Sell your hook. Know your scope.

Pitch mistakes

(Mostly from ‚Äúhow to explain your game to an asshole‚Äù (‚ÄúGDC Talk: How To Explain Your Game To An Asshole - a Post on Tom Francis‚Äô Blog‚Äù n.d.).)

  • relying on game footage or screenshots and not explaining the game. You have no idea how others think or how they interpret things, so spell it out.
  • thinking that to explain your game, you should explain your artistic intent, e.g. if you say ‚Äúit‚Äôs a game about loss‚Äù. DOOM might be a game about loss, right? Your message/theme/artistic intent won‚Äôt tell them anything about how to play the game or what makes it different.
  • (mentioned earlier) Thinking your story explains the game. It doesn‚Äôt.
  • saying it‚Äôs innovative. You: ‚Äúmy game is innovative‚Äù. Them: ‚Äúwow, that sounds really innovative‚Äù. Unlikely, right?

Technical research

The UX sub-team have done a bunch of their own user research, and it‚Äôs looking good. The insights which are particularly important to me are that the users we researched all own a mobile device, which is what we‚Äôre targeting for our experience.

Also, more of them listen than read (although the vast majority of them watch, i.e. film and TV), but the fact that they are listeners of podcasts and music is good for us as we‚Äôre delivering an audio-first experience.

As for play-testing, I conducted some very light conceptual testing with friends and family who are already audio-book and podcast consumers, showing them the Twine demo on my mobile device, and getting them to listen and interact with it.

All of them liked the demo. The biggest feature request was for the game to select a random path after a timeout, just in case they‚Äôre listening to the experience whilst driving or doing the laundry.

I did not implement this feature in the Twine demo, but I will push for the Unity team (aka Oli) to implement this in our Unity-based final product.
